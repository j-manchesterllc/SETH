Here is a detailed, actionable guide to building a private market opportunity curation business from absolute zero.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-3)

### Week 1-2: Legal & Regulatory Groundwork

Your first step isn't finding a deal—it's ensuring you won't be breaking the law when you do. The finder vs. broker-dealer distinction is your primary legal concern.

**Action Steps:**

1. **Retain a securities attorney.** Find a lawyer who specializes in private placements and broker-dealer regulation. Expect to pay $500-1,500 for an initial consultation and agreement drafting.
2. **Get your documents drafted:**
   - **Non-Circumvent, Non-Disclosure, Non-Solicit (NNN) Agreement.** This is non-negotiable. It prevents capital partners from cutting you out of deals and protects the confidentiality of your deal flow. An NNN agreement surpasses the scope of a standard non-disclosure agreement by addressing the specific risks inherent in these collaborations. Never share a deal name or specifics before this is signed.
   - **Finder/Advisory Agreement.** This contract between you and the capital source (or issuer) explicitly states you are not a registered broker-dealer, defines your limited role as making introductions, and outlines your success fee structure.
3. **Research state law.** If you're in California, Texas, or New York, your regulatory risk is higher. Be aware of your state's specific requirements for finders.

### Week 3-4: Build Your Central Intelligence File (CIF)

This is your core asset. Start with a simple, encrypted structure (a password-protected spreadsheet is fine initially, but plan to migrate to an encrypted CRM like Salesforce Shield or Affinity).

**Action Steps:**

1. **Populate "Capital Sources" sheet:** Identify 100+ family offices, private credit funds, and secondary funds. Use free resources like LinkedIn, Crunchbase, and SEC filings. For each, record:
   - Investment mandate (sector, stage, geography)
   - Typical check size
   - Key decision-makers and their backgrounds
   - Recent deals they've done (publicly announced)
2. **Populate "Originators" sheet:** Identify 100+ professionals who see deals before they're public. These include:
   - Restructuring and turnaround advisors
   - M&A attorneys at middle-market law firms
   - Boutique investment bankers
   - Fund formation lawyers
   - Commercial bankers in special assets groups

## Phase 2: Intelligence & Relationship Building (Months 4-6)

Your goal here is not to pitch deals. It's to become a known, trusted, and valuable contact within your target ecosystem.

### Month 4: Provide Value First

**Action Steps:**

1. **Create a "Market Intelligence" digest.** Spend a few hours each week curating news on a specific niche (e.g., "GP-led secondaries in the industrial sector"). Email this digest to 20-30 originators from your CIF. Do not ask for anything in return. The subject line should be simple: "Recent industrial GP-led activity."
2. **Engage on LinkedIn.** Follow the people on your CIF list. Comment thoughtfully on their posts. Share relevant articles with your own brief analysis.

### Month 5: The First Outreach

**Action Steps:**

1. **Contact 20-30 originators.** Your opening line is critical. Do not say, "I'm a finder looking for deals." Instead, use a variation of: *"I'm researching the lower-middle-market GP-led space and noticed your firm's work in [sector]. I'm building a network of capital partners focused on this area and would be happy to share my notes on recent secondary transactions if it's helpful."*
2. **Record everything in your CIF.** After every call or email, log the response. Did they engage? What did they mention? This is your "pattern library."
3. **Set a weekly goal:** Aim for 5-10 new, substantive conversations per week.

### Month 6: Cultivating Early-Stage Deal Flow

By now, a few originators should be responsive. You might hear about a "situation" that isn't yet a deal.

**Action Steps:**

1. **Ask the right question:** When an originator mentions a challenging situation, ask: *"What's the specific friction preventing this from being a simple process?"* This positions you as a problem-solver, not a commission-seeker.
2. **Map relationships:** In your CIF, start drawing connections between the capital preferences you've documented and the friction points originators are describing.

## Phase 3: Capital & Deal Pursuit (Months 7-12)

This is where you shift from passive intelligence gathering to active deal pursuit.

### Month 7-8: Capital Source Outreach

**Action Steps:**

1. **Contact 20-30 capital sources** (family offices, private credit funds). Use a similar value-first approach.
2. **Ask the crucial question:** *"What specific type of opportunity do you want to see that you never seem to get offered?"*
3. **Document the response verbatim in your CIF.** This is your matchmaking key.
4. **Key Finding:** Club deals now account for up to 69% of family office investments overall, rising to 83% in the venture space. This is your entry point—family offices are actively seeking co-investment partners.

### Month 9-10: Identifying a Target Deal

You're looking for a deal that matches the criteria you've documented from capital sources. Focus on these three "ugly" categories:

**Category 1: GP-Led Secondaries (Lower Middle Market)**
GP-led volume reached ~$104 billion in 2025, a 50% YoY increase. Only 31% of mid-cap GPs have ever completed a GP-led secondary, creating a massive need for guidance. Look for funds holding companies past their 10-year term needing to provide LP liquidity.

**Category 2: Credit-Stressed Special Situations**
Private credit default rates increased to 5-6% in early 2026, compared with 1-3% historically. PIK loan usage is at 11%. This creates a pipeline of fundamentally sound businesses with temporary covenant breaches.

**Category 3: Essential Industrial Assets**
Family businesses with no succession plan, "boring" but cash-flowing assets, or companies with single-customer concentration that scare off institutional buyers.

**Action Steps:**

1. **Vet the opportunity.** Is the friction genuine? Is there a structural solution? Can you articulate it in one page?
2. **Prepare a Situation Brief.** This is not a pitch deck. It's a one-page, factual document that explains the asset (anonymized), the specific friction, and the required structural solution. Use only verifiable statements. Example: *"The company's EBITDA has grown at a CAGR of 12% over three years. The covenant breach was triggered by a temporary supply chain disruption that has since been resolved."* This is curation, not investment advice.

### Month 11-12: The First Close Attempt

**Action Steps:**

1. **Present to 1-3 pre-vetted capital sources.** Choose only those whose stated preferences align perfectly with the opportunity.
2. **Facilitate the introduction.** This is a crucial, hands-off step. You introduce the parties via email, stating clearly: *"I'm connecting you based on our prior conversations. [Capital Source] is interested in [Friction Type]. [Originator] is handling a situation involving [Asset Description]. I'm not advising either party. Please connect directly."*
3. **Step back.** Do not participate in negotiations. Do not discuss valuation. Your role is complete.
4. **Monitor the process.** You can check in to ensure the connection was made, but your work is done.
5. **Success Fee.** If the deal closes, you collect your 3-8% success fee. If it doesn't, log the lessons learned in your pattern library and start the cycle again.

## Phase 4: Systematization & First Deal (Months 13-18)

The first deal is the hardest. It's a proof of existence. Once you have one close, you have a track record. Use this phase to close 2-3 more deals using the exact same process.

**Action Steps:**

1. **Form a closed Syndicate** of the 5-8 capital partners you've successfully transacted with.
2. **Offer a Right of First Refusal (ROFR)** on new opportunities in exchange for a monthly retainer ($5k-$10k, credited against success fees).
3. **Launch a paid quarterly intelligence report** ($10k-$25k/year) with anonymized case studies and pattern analysis. This creates recurring revenue and establishes you as a trusted advisor.

### The Transition to Independent Sponsor (Months 18-24+)

After 3+ closed deals, you can transition to the principal model. You will source and structure deals yourself, form an SPV, and offer co-investment slices to your syndicate. You'll charge a closing fee (1-2% of enterprise value), a management fee (3-5% of EBITDA annually), and carried interest (20% of profits above an 8-10% preferred return).

## Critical Resources & Final Advice

- **Do not use free templates.** The cost of a poorly drafted NNN or Finder Agreement is not the legal fee you save; it's the deal you lose or the enforcement action you face.
- **Capital required:** You need 12-24 months of living expenses saved. You will not generate meaningful revenue quickly.
- **If you get stuck:** Re-evaluate your deal selection criteria, capital matching, and originator relationships. Adjust and run the cycle again. Persistence is the primary differentiator.

This is the map. The journey is yours.
