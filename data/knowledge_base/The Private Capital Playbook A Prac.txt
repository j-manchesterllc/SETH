### **Section 1: The Mechanics of Value Concentration**

Understanding how value concentrates in competitive systems is the foundation upon which all successful intermediation is built. The following mechanics describe patterns observed across multiple market cycles, asset classes, and geographies.

**1.1 Position Determines Compensation**

In any transaction chain, returns accumulate at control points—bottlenecks, dependencies, unique access points—not at points of labor. Effort is abundant and replaceable. Positional control is scarce.

A junior investment banker working 100 hours per week on a transaction will not out-earn a managing director who works 20 hours but controls the client relationship and deal flow. The difference is not effort. It is position. The MD owns a dependency in the transaction chain that cannot be easily bypassed.

**Practical Application:** As an intermediary, your goal is to become a control point. This means developing relationships with originators who see deals before they become public, and with capital sources who trust your curation. When both sides need you to access the other, you have position.

**1.2 Friction Creates Premium**

Problems people are reluctant to discuss publicly, or desires they deny, carry higher barriers to entry and lower competition. The gap between public statement and private behavior is where margins are largest.

In private markets, the friction is often structural rather than moral. A general partner with a fund approaching the end of its term cannot publicly market a portfolio company without signaling distress. A family business founder with no succession plan cannot announce a sale without destabilizing the workforce. A regional bank under regulatory pressure cannot openly solicit rescue financing for a borrower in covenant breach. These are the "ugly" deals—and they carry premium pricing precisely because most institutional buyers avoid them.

**Practical Application:** Train yourself to identify friction points. When you hear an originator describe a situation as "complex," "sensitive," or "confidential," you are hearing the sound of an opportunity.

**1.3 Timing Is the Multiplier**

Knowledge generates economic return only at the moment of need. Free, widely available information has near-zero value. The same information, delivered precisely when a counterparty is stuck, desperate, or facing a deadline, has measurable value.

**Practical Application:** Build systems that surface opportunities at moments of acute need. A covenant breach, a fund term expiration, a key-person event—these are timing triggers that compress decision timelines and increase the value of a well-timed solution.

**1.4 Recurrence Over Transaction**

Embedding yourself into a counterparty's ongoing process creates structural necessity and recurring value. One-off transactions require repeated re-acquisition of the customer.

**Practical Application:** Design your business model to create recurring touchpoints. A quarterly intelligence report, a retainer-based syndicate, or a management agreement all convert one-time transactions into ongoing relationships.

**1.5 Framework Control Shapes Outcomes**

The party that sets the criteria for evaluation shapes the outcome before any negotiation begins. In a hiring process, the candidate who defines what "qualified" means controls the interview. In an M&A process, the party that defines what "synergy" means controls the valuation discussion.

**Practical Application:** Your Situation Brief—the one-page document that frames the opportunity—is your primary tool for establishing the evaluation framework. Frame the opportunity around the specific friction point and the structural solution, not around generic investment merits.

**1.6 Volatility Transfers Assets**

During periods of instability, most actors freeze. Those who act decisively acquire at discounted prices. This is a mechanical effect of asymmetric reaction times, observable in every market dislocation.

**Practical Application:** The current market environment—characterized by record dry powder, historically low distribution rates, and a surge in GP-led secondary transactions—is a period of instability. This is precisely when opportunistic intermediaries can capture value.

**1.7 Irreplaceability Sets the Ceiling**

If removal of a participant causes systemic disruption, that participant captures value. If removal is consequence-free, that participant is a commodity.

**Practical Application:** A finder who merely forwards a deal memo is replaceable. A finder who has vetted the opportunity, structured the Situation Brief, pre-qualified the capital source, and facilitated a confidential process is difficult to replace. An independent sponsor who has sourced, structured, and co-invested in a deal is very difficult to replace. Your goal is to move from commodity to dependency.

### **Section 2: Market Dynamics — The 2026 Landscape**

The structural opening for a curated opportunity business is not theoretical. It is measurable. The following data points describe the current market conditions as of early-to-mid 2026.

**2.1 Dry Powder: Capital Under Deployment Pressure**

Global private capital dry powder has reached unprecedented levels, creating intense pressure on general partners to deploy capital. Global private equity dry powder across all strategies stands at approximately $3.7 trillion as of early 2026, with buyout dry powder specifically accounting for roughly $1.2 trillion. Notably, 24% of buyout dry powder has been held for over four years. The fundraising environment has tightened considerably—global PE fundraising in 2025 was approximately $780 billion, down from a peak of roughly $1.1 trillion in 2021. An estimated 29,000 unsold portfolio companies represent approximately $3.6 trillion of trapped value.

The capital concentration at the top is stark: 45% of all capital raised goes to less than 5% of funds. This means the vast majority of GPs—particularly in the lower middle market—are starved for attention from institutional limited partners. They need liquidity solutions. They need partners. They need intermediaries.

**2.2 Secondary Market: A Structural Shift**

The secondary market for private assets has exploded, reaching record levels in 2025 with continued growth projected for 2026. Total volume reached between $226-233 billion in 2025, with projections pointing toward $250 billion or more in 2026. GP-led transactions—continuation funds, tender offers, and other liquidity structures—reached $104-116 billion in total volume, representing approximately 50% year-over-year growth.

Continuation funds now represent over 10% of sponsor-backed exit volume for three consecutive years. Critically, only 31% of mid-cap GPs have ever completed a GP-led secondary, compared to 67% of large-cap GPs. This penetration gap in the lower middle market represents a significant opportunity for intermediaries who can guide inexperienced GPs through the process and source capital from family offices and secondary funds targeting this segment.

**2.3 Family Offices: The Rise of Direct Investing**

Family offices have transformed from passive limited partners to active direct investors, fundamentally reshaping the private capital landscape. More than 70% of family offices now participate in direct private deals, with 40% reporting increased direct activity over the past year. Approximately 37% plan to increase private equity allocations in the coming 18 months.

The club deal has become the dominant structure for family office co-investment. Approximately 69% of all family office transactions are now structured as club deals, and for venture deals specifically, that figure rises to 83%. Family offices prefer a streamlined process: a concise investment memo and a clean SPV structure ready for execution.

This shift creates a structural opening for intermediaries. Family offices have the mandate and capital for direct investing but lack the dedicated sourcing networks of institutional funds. They rely on trusted intermediaries to source and vet opportunities.

**2.4 Private Credit: Distress Creates Opportunity**

The private credit market, which grew explosively over the past decade, is experiencing its first major stress test. Default rates have risen significantly, with some portfolios reporting rates as high as 9.2% in 2025 compared to a pre-2021 average of 1-3%. PIK (payment-in-kind) loan usage has increased to 11% of the private credit market, up from 5% in 2022.

Distressed and special situations fundraising reached $56.9 billion in 2025, signaling strong institutional appetite for rescue capital. Sectors under particular stress include automotive, manufacturing, and construction.

This environment creates a pipeline of rescue financing opportunities: fundamentally sound businesses facing temporary liquidity crunches, covenant breaches triggered by short-term disruptions, and regional banks under regulatory pressure to reduce non-performing exposure. These situations are time-sensitive, complex, and often too small for institutional distressed funds—ideal for a relationship-based intermediary who can move quickly.

**2.5 The Independent Sponsor: An Asset Class Emerges**

Independent sponsors—also called fundless sponsors—source and execute transactions one deal at a time without raising a blind pool of capital. What was once an opportunistic niche has evolved into a defined asset class with converging market standards.

Surveys of independent sponsor transactions reveal the following economics: transaction or closing fees of 1-2% of enterprise value at closing, management or monitoring fees of 3-5% of portfolio company EBITDA annually, carried interest of 20-25% of profits above a preferred return hurdle of 8-10% annually. The "two and twenty" structure (2% management fee, 20% carry) is used in approximately 58% of deals. Capital providers increasingly expect meaningful equity participation from the sponsor—skin in the game—which has driven many sponsors to raise working capital lines or partner with family offices on shared GP economics.

### **Section 3: The Opportunity Curation Model**

**3.1 Core Definition**

An opportunity curation business is a private, invitation-only intermediary service that:
1. Sources investment opportunities that are not publicly marketed
2. Vets them for structural viability (not investment merit—see Section 4 for regulatory nuance)
3. Matches them with pre-qualified capital sources
4. Captures economics through success fees, retainers, or carried interest

The venture does not handle client funds, does not provide regulated investment advice, and operates within established finder frameworks.

**3.2 Opportunity Categories**

Three categories consistently produce off-market opportunities in the current environment:

**GP-Led Secondaries (Lower Middle Market, $10M-$50M Enterprise Value)**
The friction is complex valuation, multi-party negotiation, and confidentiality requirements. The GP cannot run a public auction without signaling distress, yet only 31% of mid-cap GPs have ever completed a GP-led secondary. They need guidance and a trusted intermediary who can bring a single-family office or small syndicate to the table without a public process.

**Credit-Stressed Special Situations**
The friction is time sensitivity, regulatory pressure on lenders, and the stigma of distress. Regional banks under regulatory pressure are accelerating defaults. Private credit funds with high PIK usage are facing covenant breaches. The business is often fundamentally sound but temporarily over-levered. Speed is the primary value driver.

**Essential Industrial / Legacy Assets**
The friction is the absence of a "growth" narrative, family succession issues, or single-customer concentration that scares off institutional buyers. These businesses often have 15-20 year operating histories, stable EBITDA, and require hands-on operational management. Family offices seeking yield and downside protection are ideal counterparties.

**3.3 The Four-Phase Operating Model**

**Phase A: Intelligence Assembly (6-12 Months)**
Build a Central Intelligence File (CIF)—an encrypted database containing:
- Capital source profiles: sector preference, ticket size, return hurdle, decision-maker psychology, and what specific situation they want to see but never get offered
- Originator profiles: lawyers, turnaround advisors, boutique bankers, restructuring specialists who see proprietary situations before they become public
- Pattern library: past deals, rejection reasons, structural solutions that closed them, outcome data

This phase requires patience. You cannot cold-call originators and expect results. You must provide value first—share market intelligence, offer relevant introductions, demonstrate competence.

**Phase B: First Close (Months 7-18)**
1. Source one deal from an originator in your CIF
2. Prepare a one-page Situation Brief including: asset description (anonymized), specific friction preventing public process, required structural solution
3. Present to no more than three pre-vetted capital sources
4. Facilitate introduction without negotiating pricing or terms
5. Collect success fee upon close

The first close validates your model. After this transaction, you are no longer hypothetical.

**Phase C: Systematization (Months 13-24)**
After 2-3 closed deals:
- Form a closed Syndicate of 5-8 capital partners who have transacted with you
- Present new opportunities only to the syndicate
- Offer a Right of First Refusal mandate with a retainer fee credited against success fees
- Create a paid quarterly intelligence report with anonymized case studies and pattern analysis

**Phase D: Principal Model (Months 20-36)**
- Identify and structure opportunities yourself using CIF insights
- Offer co-investment slices to your syndicate
- Charge carried interest (typically 20% of profits) plus management fee
- Form Special Purpose Vehicles for each deal, with you as manager

The transition from finder to independent sponsor changes the economics by an order of magnitude.

### **Section 4: Legal and Regulatory Framework**

**4.1 The Finder / Broker-Dealer Distinction**

Under federal securities laws, any person "engaged in the business of effecting transactions in securities for the account of others" must register as a broker-dealer. Courts and the SEC apply a facts-and-circumstances test considering:
- Transaction-based compensation: Receiving a success fee tied to a securities transaction is a strong indicator of broker-dealer activity
- Participation in negotiations: The more you participate in negotiating price or terms, the more likely you are a broker-dealer
- Handling of funds or securities: Touching client money is a bright-line prohibition
- Solicitation of investors: Actively seeking out and recommending specific investments
- Regularity of activity: A one-time introduction is less likely to trigger registration than an ongoing business

The SEC has acknowledged that purely ministerial introductions do not require broker-dealer registration, but the line is fact-specific.

**4.2 Current Regulatory Status (2026)**

As of early 2026, no federal safe harbor exists for individuals who connect companies with accredited investors and receive compensation. The SEC's October 2020 proposal was never finalized. In July 2025, the SEC signaled it could revive the proposal, but no rule has been adopted.

On March 17, 2026, De Silva Law Offices filed a formal rulemaking petition with the SEC requesting a finder's exemption. The petition argues that the absence of an exemption is a structural barrier to capital formation, that finders and broker-dealers serve categorically different markets, and that the current uncertainty falls hardest on founders who lack inherited social networks. The petition is pending; do not base your business model on its adoption.

**4.3 State Law Considerations**

State laws vary significantly. California, Texas, and New York aggressively pursue unregistered finders. Other states are more permissive. Some have explicit finder exemptions; most do not. You must check each jurisdiction where capital sources or opportunity owners are located.

**4.4 Operational Guidelines**

Based on current regulatory practice and no-action letters, you can operate as a finder if you make introductions between parties, provide factual and anonymized information, receive a success fee (subject to state law), work exclusively with accredited investors, and use properly drafted written agreements. You must not participate in negotiations, provide investment advice or recommendations, handle client funds or securities, solicit the general public, or operate without documentation.

**4.5 Essential Legal Documents**

Engage qualified securities counsel to prepare: a Non-Circumvent, Non-Disclosure, Non-Solicit (NNN) Agreement to protect deal sources and client relationships; a Finder/Advisory Agreement to define role, compensation, and liability limitations; Confidentiality Protocols to establish information barriers; and an SPV Operating Agreement (Phase D) to govern co-investment vehicles.

**4.6 Risk Mitigation**

Document everything. Every email, call summary, and introduction. If you are ever questioned by a regulator, your documentary record should show that you made an introduction, provided factual information, and stepped back. It should not show that you recommended the investment, negotiated terms, or touched money.

### **Section 5: Execution Roadmap**

**Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-6)**

| Month | Focus | Key Activities |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| 1-2 | Legal Infrastructure | Retain securities counsel; draft NNN and Finder Agreements; research state law in target jurisdictions |
| 3-4 | Intelligence Infrastructure | Build encrypted CIF; define data schema; begin populating with public information on 100+ capital sources and 100+ originators |
| 5-6 | Originator Outreach | Contact 20-30 originators; provide value first; ask what deals on their desk are fundamentally sound but too complex for a public process |

By the end of Month 6, aim to have 5-10 active, responsive originator relationships and 1-3 early-stage deal situations in view.

**Phase 2: First Deal Pursuit (Months 7-18)**

| Month | Focus | Key Activities |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| 7-9 | Capital Source Outreach | Contact 20-30 capital sources; ask what specific situation they want to see but never get offered; record risk appetite and decision-making process |
| 10-12 | Deal Identification | Identify one target deal from originator conversations that matches capital source preferences; vet for structural viability; prepare Situation Brief |
| 13-18 | First Close | Present to 1-3 capital sources; facilitate introduction; monitor process; drive toward close |

The first deal is the hardest. You have no track record. Capital sources are skeptical. Originators are protective. This phase may take longer than projected. The only objective is progress toward a single closed transaction.

**Phase 3: Systematization (Months 19-30)**

| Activity | Target Timing |
|----------|---------------|
| Close 2-3 additional deals | Months 19-24 |
| Form closed Syndicate (5-8 capital partners) | Month 24 |
| Launch quarterly intelligence report | Month 25 |
| Transition to principal model (first SPV) | Months 26-30 |

**Phase 4: Scaling (Year 3 and Beyond)**
- Build a team to expand origination coverage
- Develop sector specialization based on your pattern library
- Raise a dedicated fund (optional—many independent sponsors remain fundless by choice)
- Establish formal capital partner relationships with multiple family offices

**Critical Guidance**
If no close occurs by Month 18, do not abandon the model. Re-evaluate your deal selection, capital matching, originator relationships, and regulatory posture. Adjust and run the cycle again.

### **Section 6: Case Studies in Action**

The following case studies are constructed from real deal structures and outcomes documented in industry reports.

**Case Study 1: GP-Led Secondary in Healthcare IT**

**Situation:** A $25 million private equity fund had held a healthcare IT portfolio company for seven years, exceeding the fund's 10-year term. The company was performing well (15% EBITDA growth annually) but could not be sold in a public auction due to confidentiality constraints.

**Friction:** The GP needed liquidity for limited partners but a full auction would signal distress. Traditional secondary buyers required extensive due diligence the company could not accommodate while operating. The GP had never completed a GP-led secondary.

**Solution:** The intermediary identified a single-family office with a healthcare technology mandate and willingness to accept a minority stake alongside a continuation vehicle. The Situation Brief highlighted operational strength and explained why a confidential process served both parties.

**Outcome:** The family office committed $8 million. The continuation vehicle closed at a valuation 20% above the fund's last reported NAV. Success fee: 5% ($400,000). Time from introduction to close: 4 months.

**Key Learning:** The deal closed because the intermediary matched a specific capital source to a specific friction point. No auction. No marketing. Pure relational value.

**Case Study 2: Rescue Financing in Manufacturing**

**Situation:** A manufacturing business with $15 million revenue and $2 million EBITDA faced a covenant breach due to temporary supply chain disruption. The business was fundamentally sound with a 20-year operating history.

**Friction:** The senior lender was a regional bank under regulatory pressure to reduce non-performing exposure. The bank issued a default notice and accelerated the debt. The business could not qualify for new senior debt due to the default on its record.

**Solution:** The intermediary identified a private credit fund specializing in rescue financing. The Situation Brief explained the temporary nature of the disruption, the bank's regulatory constraints, and the proposed structural solution: a super-senior secured facility at 14% interest.

**Outcome:** The private credit fund provided a $5 million facility. The bank was repaid in full. The business returned to profitability within 12 months. Success fee: 6% ($300,000). Time from introduction to close: 6 weeks.

**Key Learning:** Speed was the primary value driver. The intermediary's existing relationship with the private credit fund—built over months of prior conversations—was the difference between a 6-week close and a missed opportunity.

**Case Study 3: Industrial Legacy Asset Club Deal**

**Situation:** A family-owned logistics company serving the semiconductor industry sought a successor. The founder, age 72, had no family members willing to take over. The business had $30 million revenue, $6 million EBITDA, and a 15-year contract with a major chip manufacturer.

**Friction:** The business was not "sexy"—single facility, no proprietary technology, required hands-on management. Strategic buyers would not pay a premium. The founder wanted a clean exit but not to a competitor.

**Solution:** The intermediary—now operating as an independent sponsor—identified three family offices with shared interest in industrial infrastructure and structured a club deal through an SPV. The sponsor committed $250,000 of personal capital, rolled the transaction fee into the deal, and negotiated a 5% management fee plus 20% carried interest above a 10% preferred return.

**Outcome:** The club deal closed at $25 million enterprise value. Estimated carried interest value over the hold period: $1-2 million. Time from sourcing to close: 8 months.

**Key Learning:** The move from finder to co-sponsor changed the economics by an order of magnitude. The sponsor's personal capital commitment and operational oversight responsibilities were material—this is not a passive role.

**Case Study 4: VC Growth Secondary**

**Situation:** A venture capital fund had held a late-stage enterprise software company for 5 years. The company was growing at 25% annually but not yet ready for IPO. The VC fund was approaching the end of its 10-year term.

**Friction:** The GP-led VC/growth market saw record expansion in 2025 with over $12 billion of volume, but many secondary buyers still focus on buyout. The VC fund needed a specialized secondary buyer.

**Solution:** The intermediary identified a secondary fund with a dedicated VC/growth continuation vehicle strategy. The Situation Brief highlighted recurring revenue metrics and the GP's continued involvement post-transaction.

**Outcome:** The continuation vehicle closed at a valuation in line with the company's last funding round. Success fee: 5% ($250,000). Time from introduction to close: 5 months.

**Key Learning:** Specialization matters. The intermediary's knowledge of the emerging VC/growth secondary market—a niche within a niche—was the key to matching capital to opportunity.

### **Section 8: Frequently Asked Questions**

**What is the difference between a finder and a broker-dealer?**
A finder makes introductions. A broker-dealer negotiates terms, handles funds, provides investment advice, and is registered with the SEC and FINRA. Key factors: transaction-based compensation, participation in negotiations, handling of funds, solicitation of investors, and regularity of activity.

**What is a typical success fee?**
For debt placements, 1-5% (typically 2-3%). For equity placements, 3-10% (typically 5-7%). For lower middle market transactions ($10M-$50M), 5-8%. For larger transactions, 2-5%.

**Do I need a retainer?**
For a first deal, a pure success fee is typical. For ongoing relationships, a monthly retainer of $5,000-$10,000 credited against success fees is observed. After establishing a track record (3+ closed deals), a retainer-based syndicate model becomes viable.

**How do I protect my deal sources?**
Use a Non-Circumvent, Non-Disclosure, Non-Solicit (NNN) Agreement before disclosing any confidential information. Include a liquidated damages clause. The agreement must be signed by all parties before any deal-specific information is shared.

**How long does a typical deal take?**
Timeframes range from 6 weeks (simple rescue financings) to 8 months (complex club deals). The average for lower middle market transactions is 3-6 months. First deals typically take longer—6-12 months from first conversation to close.

**What is the minimum viable deal size?**
For the finder model to be economically viable, minimum transaction size is typically $5 million in capital deployed, yielding a success fee of $150,000-$400,000 at 3-8%. For smaller deals, a higher percentage fee (8-10%) may be negotiated.

**How much personal capital do I need?**
At minimum: 12-24 months of living expenses. For Phase D (independent sponsor), you will need capital for skin in the game—typically $100,000-$500,000 per deal. Many independent sponsors raise working capital lines or partner with family offices on shared GP economics.

**What if the SEC adopts a finder exemption?**
The De Silva petition is pending but do not wait for regulatory clarity to start building relationships. Even if adopted, any exemption would likely come with conditions—limits on compensation, disclosure requirements, and restrictions on investor types.

### **Section 10: Emerging Frontiers**

Beyond the core opportunity categories described in Section 3, several emerging frontiers offer additional paths for practitioners who have established a track record and relationships.

**10.1 Continuation Funds for Growth-Stage Companies**
The GP-led secondary market is expanding beyond traditional buyout into growth equity and venture capital. In 2025, GP-led VC/growth volume exceeded $12 billion, representing 40%+ year-over-year growth. This segment remains underserved by institutional secondary buyers, many of whom lack expertise in valuing growth-stage companies with negative EBITDA but strong recurring revenue metrics. For intermediaries with technology or growth equity backgrounds, this represents a specialized niche with less competition and higher potential fees.

**10.2 NAV Loans and Preferred Equity Solutions**
Net Asset Value (NAV) loans—financing secured by a fund's portfolio of assets—have emerged as a significant liquidity tool for GPs seeking to return capital to LPs without selling assets at inopportune times. The NAV lending market has grown substantially, with private credit funds and specialized lenders providing capital at the fund level. Intermediaries who understand fund-level financing structures can source opportunities where a GP needs liquidity but prefers a NAV facility over a continuation fund. These transactions often involve multiple stakeholders and complex negotiations, creating room for advisory fees.

**10.3 GP Stakes and Seeding Transactions**
The market for minority investments in private equity firms—GP stakes—has matured into a distinct asset class. Established firms sell minority interests to fund succession, expand into new strategies, or provide liquidity to founding partners. For emerging managers, seed capital from anchor investors can accelerate fundraising and provide working capital during the critical early years. Intermediaries with relationships among both GPs and institutional investors (including family offices seeking GP exposure) can facilitate these transactions, earning placement fees or advisory retainers.

**10.4 Cross-Border Family Office Syndication**
Family offices in different geographies often have complementary investment objectives and sector expertise. A European family office with industrial heritage may seek co-investors for a manufacturing deal but lack relationships with US-based families. An Asian family office with technology focus may want exposure to US growth-stage companies but lack local sourcing capabilities. Intermediaries who can bridge these geographic gaps—understanding cultural nuances, legal frameworks, and investment preferences across regions—can build valuable cross-border syndication practices.

**10.5 Distressed Fund Interests**
The LP-led secondary market, which grew 34% in 2025, includes a subset of transactions involving distressed fund interests: LP stakes in underperforming funds or funds with significant unfunded commitments. These trades often occur at steep discounts to NAV, but require sophisticated analysis of the underlying portfolio and fund-level dynamics. Intermediaries who develop expertise in valuing distressed fund interests can source opportunities from LPs seeking immediate liquidity (even at a discount) and match them with secondary buyers willing to underwrite complex situations.

### **Conclusion**

The private capital markets have never been larger, more complex, or more fragmented. Global dry powder exceeds $3.7 trillion. The secondary market is projected to reach $250 billion in 2026.
